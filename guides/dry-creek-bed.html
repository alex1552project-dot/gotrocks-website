How to Build a Dry Creek Bed

Turn drainage problems into stunning landscape features. A dry creek bed channels rainwater away from your home while adding natural beauty to your yardâ€”functional art that works even when it's dry.

ğŸ“‹ Step-by-Step Overview

1

Map the Water Flow

Watch where water naturally flows during rain. Mark the path from the problem area (like a downspout or low spot) to where you want water to exit. Natural curves look more realistic than straight lines.

2

Dig the Channel

Excavate 6-12 inches deep and 18-36 inches wide. Wider looks more natural. Create a gentle slope (1" drop per 10 feet) and make the center slightly deeper than the edges for realistic water flow.

3

Line with Landscape Fabric

Lay heavy-duty landscape fabric along the entire channel, extending 6-12 inches up the sides. This prevents weeds and keeps rocks from sinking into soil over time.

4

Place Large Anchor Stones

Position your largest Bull Rock or boulders along the edges and at curves. These "anchor stones" create natural-looking banks and prevent erosion. Bury them 1/3 deep for stability.

5

Fill with River Rock

Add medium Bull Rock (3-5") in the center channel. Vary sizes for a natural look. Larger rocks at edges, smaller toward the center mimics how real streams sort stones.

6

Add Finishing Touches

Fill gaps with Pea Gravel or small river rock. Add native plants along the edgesâ€”ornamental grasses, ferns, or sedges. A few larger accent boulders complete the look.

ğŸ’¡ Pro Tips from Texas Got Rocks

  • Think in curves: Real streams never flow straight. Add gentle S-curves and vary the width to create a natural appearance.
  • Mix rock sizes: Use at least 3 different sizes. Uniform rocks look artificialâ€”nature is random.
  • Create pools: Widen the creek at a few spots to create "pool" areas where water can collect briefly.
  • Add a bridge: A simple flagstone or wooden plank bridge adds charm and function if the creek crosses a path.
  • Plant strategically: Ornamental grasses hide fabric edges and soften the transition between rock and lawn.
