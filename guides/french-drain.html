How to Build a French Drain

Stop water from pooling in your yard. A French drain redirects groundwater away from problem areas using a simple gravel-filled trench. Here's exactly how to do it‚Äîand what you'll need.

üìã Step-by-Step Overview

1

Plan Your Route

Identify where water collects and map a path to direct it away from structures. Ensure a consistent downward slope of at least 1% (1 inch drop per 8 feet). Water always flows downhill‚Äîuse a string level to verify grade.

2

Call 811 & Dig the Trench

Always call 811 before digging to locate buried utilities‚Äîit's free and required by law. Dig your trench 12-18 inches deep and 6-12 inches wide. Maintain consistent slope throughout.

3

Line & Add Base Layer

Line the trench with landscape fabric (this prevents soil from clogging your drain over time). Add 2-3 inches of Limestone Base at the bottom for optimal drainage.

4

Install Perforated Pipe

Place 4-inch perforated PVC or corrugated drain pipe with holes facing DOWN (this is critical‚Äîholes up is a common mistake). Connect sections and route to your discharge point (daylight outlet, dry well, or storm drain).

5

Backfill with Gravel

Cover the pipe with more Limestone Base until the trench is nearly full. Leave 2-3 inches from the surface. Wrap the landscape fabric over the top to prevent soil intrusion.

6

Finish the Surface

Top with decorative gravel (like Pea Gravel or Bull Rock), sod, or mulch depending on your landscape design. Mark the drain path so you don't accidentally dig into it later.

üí° Pro Tips from Texas Got Rocks

  • Overestimate material: Order 10-15% more gravel than calculated. Running short mid-project is frustrating and costly.
  • Test before backfilling: Pour water into the drain and verify it flows to your outlet before covering everything up.
  • Consider a clean-out: Install a vertical pipe at bends so you can flush the system if it ever clogs.
  • Depth matters: In Texas clay soil, go deeper (18"+) to get below the freeze line and improve drainage.
  • Slope is everything: Even a slight dip creates a pooling point. Check slope every 4-6 feet while digging.
